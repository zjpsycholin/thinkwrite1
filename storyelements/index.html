Week 9 Learning Notes: Story Elements

These notes are designed to help you prepare before class and review after class. Use them to check your understanding of key concepts.

Learning Outcomes

After completing Week 9, you should be able to:

  • Identify and analyze the four core story elements: character, setting, conflict, and theme
  • Distinguish between internal and external conflict
  • Explain how setting influences character and plot
  • Recognize theme as meaning shown through story, not stated as a moral
  • Analyze a short narrative for its use of story elements
  • Apply these elements to your own narrative writing

Use this checklist to track your progress.

Key Concepts

1. Character: The Heart of Story

Definition: Characters are the people (or animals, or personified objects) that drive the story. The protagonist is the main character; the antagonist is the force opposing them.

Key Insight: Readers connect to stories through characters. We may forget plot details, but we remember characters—how they felt, what they wanted, how they changed. Strong characters make readers care what happens next.

How Character Is Revealed in Narrative:

MethodWhat It ShowsExample
ActionsWhat the character does under pressureHe gave his last coin to the beggar.
DialogueWhat the character says and how“I don’t care what they think,” she whispered.
Internal thoughtWhat the character thinks (first-person or close third)I knew I should speak up, but my throat closed.
Physical descriptionWhat the character looks likeHer hands were calloused, nails bitten to the quick.
Reactions of othersHow other characters respondEveryone stepped back when he entered the room.

Example from Literature: Revealing Character Through Action

From James Joyce’s “Araby” (first-person narrative):

“Every morning I lay on the floor in the front parlor watching her door. The blind was pulled down to within an inch of the sash so that I could not be seen. When she came out on the doorstep my heart leaped. I ran to the hall, seized my books and followed her.”

We understand the narrator’s infatuation not because he says “I was infatuated,” but because of his actions: lying on the floor, hiding, heart leaping, rushing to follow.

Character Complexity:

TypeDefinitionExample
Flat characterOne-dimensional, few traits, predictableThe evil stepmother in fairy tales
Round characterComplex, multiple traits, surprisingA character who is both brave and afraid
Static characterDoes not change during the storyA mentor figure who remains wise throughout
Dynamic characterChanges or grows as a result of eventsA selfish person who learns generosity

Note: In short personal narratives, you may not have room for full character arcs, but your narrator (you) should feel like a real person with desires and flaws.

2. Setting: The World of the Story

Definition: Setting is the time and place where the story occurs. It includes physical environment, historical period, weather, lighting, social context, and atmosphere.

Why Setting Matters: Setting is never just background. It shapes character, creates mood, influences plot, and often carries symbolic meaning. A story set in a crowded city feels different from one set on a deserted island—not just because of the scenery, but because of the possibilities and constraints each setting creates.

Functions of Setting:

FunctionExplanationExample
Creates atmosphereEstablishes mood (tense, peaceful, ominous)A foggy graveyard at midnight
Influences characterShapes who characters are and what they wantA child growing up in poverty values security
Drives plotMakes certain events possible or impossibleA snowstorm traps characters together
Carries themeSymbolizes larger ideasA decaying house represents a dying family

Example from Literature: Setting Creating Atmosphere

From Edgar Allan Poe’s “The Fall of the House of Usher”:

“During the whole of a dull, dark, and soundless day in the autumn of the year, when the clouds hung oppressively low in the heavens, I had been passing alone, on horseback, through a singularly dreary tract of country; and at length found myself, as the shades of the evening drew on, within view of the melancholy House of Usher.”

Poe establishes the mood—gloomy, oppressive, melancholic—entirely through setting details before any character speaks or any action occurs.

3. The Interaction Between Character and Setting

Character and setting do not exist separately. They shape each other.

How Setting Reveals Character:

Setting DetailWhat It Reveals
A messy, cluttered apartmentThe character may be disorganized, overwhelmed, or creative
A meticulously organized deskThe character values control, discipline, or maybe anxiety
A room with no personal itemsThe character is transient, secretive, or detached
A house filled with photographsThe character values family and memory

Example: Character Revealed Through Setting

From Charlotte Perkins Gilman’s “The Yellow Wallpaper”:

“The color is repellent, almost revolting; a smouldering unclean yellow, strangely faded by the slow-turning sunlight. It is a dull yet lurid orange in some places, a sickly sulphur tint in others.”

The narrator’s obsession with the wallpaper reveals her deteriorating mental state. The setting is not just background—it is a mirror of her mind.

How Character Shapes Setting:

Characters also influence their environments. A character who is angry might perceive a room as oppressive; a character in love might see the same room as warm and inviting.

Key principle: The same setting can feel completely different depending on the character’s emotional state. When writing from a character’s point of view, describe the setting through their eyes.

4. Conflict: The Engine of Story

Definition: Conflict is the problem or obstacle that the protagonist must face. Without conflict, there is no story—only a sequence of events.

Key Insight: Conflict creates tension, and tension makes readers want to know what happens next. A story without conflict is like a car without an engine: it might look nice, but it won’t go anywhere.

Types of Conflict:

TypeDefinitionExample
Internal conflictCharacter vs. self (fear, doubt, guilt, desire)A student must decide whether to cheat on a test
External conflictCharacter vs. outside force
— Character vs. characterProtagonist vs. another personTwo siblings fighting over an inheritance
— Character vs. natureProtagonist vs. natural forcesA hiker lost in a storm
— Character vs. societyProtagonist vs. social norms or systemsA person fighting against injustice
— Character vs. technology/fateProtagonist vs. machine or destinyA programmer trying to stop an AI

Example: Internal Conflict

From Tim O’Brien’s The Things They Carried:

“They carried the soldier’s greatest fear, which was the fear of blushing. Men killed, and died, because they were embarrassed not to. It was what had brought them to the war in the first place, nothing positive, no dreams of glory or honor, just to avoid the blush of dishonor.”

The conflict is internal: the soldier’s fear of shame drives his actions. He is not fighting the enemy; he is fighting his own embarrassment.

Conflict in Personal Narrative:

In a personal narrative, the conflict does not need to be dramatic (a car chase, a courtroom battle). It can be small but meaningful:

  • Wanting to speak but being too afraid
  • Trying to please a parent and feeling unseen
  • Making a choice between two difficult options
  • Facing the consequences of a mistake

Key question: What does the narrator want? What stands in the way?

5. Theme: The Meaning of the Story

Definition: Theme is the underlying insight or message that the story communicates about life, people, or the world. It is shown through the events of the story, not stated as a moral.

Important Distinction: A moral tells the reader what to think: “Lying is wrong.” A theme suggests something about life without preaching: “Sometimes the truth hurts more than a lie, but it is still necessary.” The reader discovers the theme through the story, not from a lecture.

How Theme Emerges:

Story ElementHow It Contributes to Theme
Character’s journeyWhat the character learns or fails to learn
Conflict and resolutionHow the problem is solved (or not)
SettingWhat the environment symbolizes
Repeating images or symbolsPatterns that suggest deeper meaning

Example: Theme Emerging from Story

From Raymond Carver’s “Cathedral” (short story):

A blind man visits a married couple. The husband is uncomfortable and prejudiced. At the end, the blind man guides the husband’s hand to draw a cathedral. The husband closes his eyes and draws, experiencing something beyond his usual vision.

The theme is not stated: “Prejudice can be overcome through shared experience.” Instead, it is shown through the husband’s transformation. Readers feel the change rather than being told about it.

Common Themes in Personal Narrative:

ThemeWhat It Explores
IdentityWho am I? How did I become this person?
FamilyWhat do we owe each other? How do we love imperfectly?
Loss and griefHow do we survive what we lose?
Coming of ageWhat does it mean to grow up?
BelongingWhere do I fit? What is home?
Failure and resilienceWhat do mistakes teach us?

Note: Do not start with a theme. Write the story honestly. The theme will emerge naturally. If you try to force a theme, the story will feel preachy and false.

Key Vocabulary

TermDefinition
CharacterThe person the story follows; revealed through action, dialogue, thought, description
SettingTime and place; creates atmosphere, influences character, drives plot
ConflictThe problem or obstacle; internal (self) or external (other, nature, society)
ThemeUnderlying insight about life; shown through story, not stated as moral
ProtagonistThe main character
AntagonistThe force opposing the protagonist
Internal conflictCharacter vs. self (fear, doubt, guilt)
External conflictCharacter vs. outside force (person, nature, society, technology)
Dynamic characterA character who changes during the story

Homework Reminder

Task: Revise your Week 8 narrative paragraph to strengthen one of the story elements (character, setting, conflict, or theme).

Requirements:

ElementRequirement
Length150-250 words
Revision focusChoose one element to develop further (add a detail, deepen the conflict, clarify the setting, etc.)
OriginalInclude your original paragraph with your revision
ReflectionWrite 2-3 sentences explaining what you changed and why

Revision suggestions:

  • Character: Add one action or line of dialogue that reveals personality
  • Setting: Add two sensory details (sound, smell, touch) that establish atmosphere
  • Conflict: Clarify what the narrator wanted and what stood in the way
  • Theme: Remove any sentence that states the meaning directly; let the story imply it

Submission: Bring original + revised paragraph + reflection to next class.
