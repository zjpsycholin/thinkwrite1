Week 4: Person Description

Learning Outcomes

After completing Week 4, you should be able to:

  • Describe a person’s physical appearance using precise, concrete language
  • Reveal character indirectly through actions, habits, and behavior
  • Use clothing, posture, and gestures to suggest personality
  • Select meaningful details that create a dominant impression of a person
  • Begin drafting Essay 1: A descriptive portrait of a significant person

Use this checklist to track your progress.

Key Concepts

1. Why Describe People?

In writing, people are not photographs. They are living presences revealed through:

ElementWhat It Reveals
Physical appearanceWhat they look like—but chosen details suggest character
BehaviorWhat they do—actions reveal who they are
SpeechWhat and how they say things
ContextTheir environment, possessions, relationships

Connection to Previous Weeks:

WeekSkillApplication to Person Description
1Concrete languageDescribe specific features, not abstract beauty
2Show, don’t tellReveal personality through action, not labels
3Sensory detailsEngage sight, sound, touch in describing people

2. Physical Description: Beyond the Mugshot

Beginning writers often describe people like this:

“She was 25 years old, with long brown hair and brown eyes. She was tall and thin and wore a blue shirt.”

This tells us facts but reveals nothing about who she is.

The Problem with Generic Description?

The Selection Principle:

You cannot describe everything. You must choose details that:

  • Create your dominant impression of the person
  • Suggest something about their character
  • Reveal their history or current state
  • Engage the reader’s imagination

Compare These Examples:

GenericRevealing
She had long brown hair.Her hair fell in a heavy braid down her back, the kind of braid that took time and care each morning.
He was old.His face was a map of lines—some deep as riverbeds, others fine as thread. Each one seemed to tell a story.
She wore a dress.She wore a dress that had been ironed with sharp creases, the kind of attention that suggested she still believed in first impressions.

Example from Literature: Charles Dickens" From Oliver Twist:

“The Jew’s villainous-looking and repulsive face was obscured by a quantity of matted red hair. He was dressed in a greasy flannel gown, with his throat bare; and seemed to be divided between sleeping and waking, pulling all the while at his pipe, which, in its long circuit from his mouth to the ground, and from the ground to his mouth, kept up a constant and soothing cloud of smoke.”

Every detail suggests something: “villainous-looking,” “matted red hair,” “greasy flannel gown”—we know this man’s character before he speaks.

Example from Literature: Tim O’Brien"
From The Things They Carried:

“Lieutenant Jimmy Cross carried letters from a girl named Martha… In the late afternoon, after a day’s march, he would dig his foxhole, wash his hands under a canteen, unwrap the letters, hold them with the tips of his fingers, and spend the last hour of light pretending.”

The detail “hold them with the tips of his fingers” reveals reverence, longing, delicacy—a man who treats these letters as sacred objects.

4. Clothing as Character Revelation

Clothing is never just clothing. It reveals:

  • Identity (who they think they are)
  • Values (what matters to them)
  • History (where they’ve been)
  • Current state (how they feel today)

Clothing Details to Notice:

CategoryQuestions to Ask
ConditionNew or worn? Clean or stained? Ironed or wrinkled?
FitTailored or baggy? Too big or too small?
StyleFashionable or dated? Practical or decorative?
CareWell-maintained or neglected?
ContextAppropriate for the situation or not?

Example from Literature: Amy Tan
From The Joy Luck Club:

“My mother wore a Chinese dress of dark blue silk, with a high collar that made her look like she was straining her neck to see over something. The dress was covered with tiny stitched flowers, each petal carefully formed. She had made it herself, by hand, in the last months before we left China.”

The dress reveals: her heritage (Chinese dress), her skill (hand-stitched flowers), her care (dark blue silk), her history (made before leaving China). We understand her without a single adjective about her personality.

Consider This Example:

He wore a suit that had been expensive once, but now the elbows shone with wear and the collar was slightly frayed. Still, the shoes were polished to a mirror shine.

What do you understand about this person?

5. The Danger of Stereotypes

Warning: Physical details can reinforce stereotypes if used carelessly.

StereotypeWhy It’s Problematic
“She was beautiful, so everyone liked her.”Equates appearance with worth
“He had a scar, so he was dangerous.”Scar = villain cliché
“She was old and frail.”Old = weak cliché
Ask: Am I using this detail because it’s true to this specific person, or because it’s what readers expect? The best details are specific and individual, not generic and predictable.

6. From Physical to Behavioral Description

Physical details show us what a person looks like. Behavioral details show us who a person is.

The Principle:

Character is revealed through choice and action, especially under pressure.

  • Habits: What they do automatically reveals personality
  • Choices: What they decide when options exist reveals values
  • Reactions: How they respond to events reveals temperament
  • Treatment of others: How they treat waiters, children, strangers reveals character

Compare:

Physical OnlyPhysical + Behavioral
She had kind eyes.She had kind eyes, and when the waiter spilled water, she was the first to say, “It’s okay, it’s just water.”

7. Actions That Reveal Character

Small Habits, Big Revelations:

ActionPossible Revelation
Arrives early to everything
Always late
Folds clothes immediately after drying
Leaves clothes in the dryer for days
Holds door for strangers
Lets doors close on people behind them

Example from Literature: James Joyce" From “Araby”:

“Every morning I lay on the floor in the front parlor watching her door. The blind was pulled down to within an inch of the sash so that I could not be seen.”

The action—lying on the floor watching, hiding behind the blind—reveals obsession, shyness, the intensity of first love.

Consider This Example:

When the argument started, my father didn’t raise his voice. He just set down his chopsticks, very carefully, and folded his hands in his lap. He looked at my mother for a long moment, then stood and walked to the window. He stood there with his back to us, looking out at the dark street, and didn’t speak again that night.

What does this reveal about the father?

8. How Someone Treats Others

One of the most revealing measures of character is how a person treats:

  • People who can do nothing for them (waiters, children, elderly)
  • People in vulnerable positions
  • People when they think no one is watching

Watch how someone treats a waiter:

  • Do they say please and thank you?
  • Do they make eye contact?
  • Do they notice when the waiter is busy?
  • Do they leave a mess or clean up after themselves?

These small interactions reveal more than any conversation.

Consider This Example:

At the crowded café, he snapped his fingers at the server. “Miss, miss! Over here.” When she came, he ordered without looking at her. After she left, he said to his companion, “They’re so slow here.” His companion said nothing, just studied the menu.

What do you understand about each person?

9. Combining Physical and Behavioral Details

The most powerful descriptions weave physical and behavioral details together.

Example from Literature: F. Scott Fitzgerald" From The Great Gatsby (describing Daisy Buchanan):

“Her face was sad and lovely with bright things in it, bright eyes and a bright passionate mouth—but there was an excitement in her voice that men who had cared for her found difficult to forget: a singing compulsion, a whispered ‘Listen,’ a promise that she had done gay, exciting things just a while since and that there were gay, exciting things hovering in the next hour.”

What physical details and behavioral details are weaven?

Example: Student Writing

My grandmother’s hands never stopped moving. Even when she sat watching television, they folded napkins into swans, smoothed the fabric of her dress, traced the pattern on the armchair. She had raised six children, and her hands still believed there was work to do.

What do we understand about the grandmother?

10. The Dominant Impression in Person Description

Just as with places, person descriptions should create a single dominant impression.

Ask yourself: What one word or phrase captures this person’s essence?

Possible Dominant Impressions for People
Dignified, restless, gentle, fierce, weary, childlike, proud, humble, guarded, open

Every detail should support this impression.

Example:
Supporting a Dominant Impression

Dominant impression: Fierce independence

  • Physical: Chin slightly raised. Shoulders squared. Hair cut short—practical, no fuss.
  • Clothing: Worn work boots, jeans with faded knees, flannel shirt with sleeves rolled.
  • Behavior: Carries her own groceries despite offers of help. When the car won’t start, she’s under the hood before anyone can call a mechanic.
  • Speech: “I’ve got it.” “No need.” “I’m fine.”

Every detail reinforces the same impression.

Practice Exercises for Self-Study

Exercise 1: Describing a Stranger

Instructions: Observe someone in your environment (a classmate, someone in a café, a person passing by). Take 5 minutes to observe. Then write a description that answers:

  • What do they look like? (specific, precise details)
  • What do their clothes suggest?
  • What does their posture or movement suggest?
  • What story might their hands tell?
  • What is your dominant impression of this person?

Do NOT:

  • Name the emotion or character trait directly
  • Invent a backstory (stick to what you observe)

Essay 1: Descriptive Portrait Assignment

Task

Write a 500-word descriptive essay about a person who has been significant in your life.

Options:

  • A family member (parent, grandparent, sibling)
  • A teacher or mentor
  • A childhood friend
  • Someone from your community
  • A person who influenced you, even briefly

Requirements

ElementRequirement
Physical descriptionAt least 3 specific, precise physical details
Behavioral descriptionAt least 2 actions or habits that reveal character
SensesAt least 3 different senses
Figurative languageAt least one simile, metaphor, or personification
Dominant impressionOne clear mood or feeling throughout
ShowingNo abstract character labels (kind, mean, shy)
StructureClear opening, body, and conclusion

Structure Suggestion

SectionContent
OpeningIntroduce the person and your relationship. Establish the dominant impression.
Physical descriptionWhat they look like—focus on details that reveal character
Behavioral descriptionWhat they do—habits, actions, ways of moving
InteractionHow they treat you or others—a specific moment or memory
ClosingReinforce the dominant impression. Suggest their significance.

Self-Check Questions:

  1. If I removed all abstract words, would a reader still understand this person?
  2. Do my physical details suggest character, not just appearance?
  3. Do my behavioral details show who they are, not just what they do?
  4. Does every detail support my dominant impression?
  5. Would someone who doesn’t know this person feel they’ve met them after reading?
