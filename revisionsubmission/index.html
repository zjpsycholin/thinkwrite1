Week 7 Learning Notes: Revision & Submission

These notes are designed to help you prepare before class and review after class. Use them to check your understanding of key concepts.

Learning Outcomes

After completing Week 7, you should be able to:

  • Identify weak or clichéd imagery in your own writing and replace it with fresh, specific details
  • Apply macro-revision strategies (structure, focus, dominant impression) before micro-revision (word choice, grammar)
  • Use sensory checklists to ensure all five senses are engaged where appropriate
  • Strengthen verb choice and eliminate abstract language
  • Prepare a final draft of the descriptive essay for submission

Use this checklist to track your progress.

Key Concepts

1. The Revision Mindset: Macro vs. Micro

Definition: Revision means “seeing again.” It is not simply fixing typos or correcting grammar. It is re-envisioning your essay to make it clearer, more vivid, and more coherent.

Two Levels of Revision:

LevelFocusQuestions to Ask
Macro-revisionBig picture: structure, focus, dominant impression, paragraph order, evidenceDoes the essay have a clear dominant impression? Is the organization logical? Does every paragraph serve a purpose?
Micro-revisionSentence level: word choice, imagery, transitions, grammar, punctuationIs every word precise? Are there clichés? Do sentences flow?

The Revision Process: Write Draft → Set Aside (few hours or a day) → Macro-revision → Micro-revision → Proofread → Final Draft

2. Strengthening Imagery: From Generic to Specific

Weak imagery relies on generic nouns, weak verbs, and clichés. Strong imagery uses precise, sensory language.

The Specificity Ladder:

GenericBetterStrongest (Specific + Sensory)
A birdA sparrowA house sparrow with its chest puffed against the cold
She laughedShe giggledShe let out a laugh that crackled like dry leaves
The room was messyThe room was clutteredClothes carpeted the floor; empty cups towered on the desk
He walkedHe shuffledHe shuffled, his slippers scuffing the linoleum

Example from Literature: From Tim O’Brien’s The Things They Carried:

“He carried a strobe light and a pair of marine binoculars. He carried a .45-caliber pistol that weighed 2.9 pounds fully loaded. He carried a compass in his rucksack and a wristwatch with a luminous dial.”

O’Brien could have said “He carried military equipment.” Instead, he gives specific weights, brands, and details. We see and feel each item.

Revision Strategy for Weak Imagery:

  1. Circle every generic noun (bird, food, building, person, thing)
  2. Ask: What specific kind? What does it look/sound/smell like?
  3. Replace with a concrete, sensory phrase.

3. Eliminating Clichés and Abstractions

Clichés are phrases that have been used so often they no longer create a vivid image.

ClichéWhy It Fails
“busy as a bee”Readers don’t picture a bee; they just register “busy”
“cold as ice”No fresh image
“heart of gold”Abstract and vague
“time stood still”Overused, loses impact

How to Revise Clichés:

  • Ask: What is a fresh way to say this?
  • Get specific: Instead of “busy as a bee,” show what the person actually does.
  • Use original comparison: Instead of “cold as ice,” describe the specific sensation of cold.

Abstract vs. Concrete:

AbstractConcrete Revision
He was nervousHis palms left damp marks on the podium.
The food was deliciousSteam rose from the dumplings. The first bite released hot broth, pork, and ginger across my tongue.
She felt lonelyShe ate dinner alone at a table for two. The chair across from her stayed empty.

4. Macro-Revision Checklist for Descriptive Essays

Before focusing on sentences, answer these big-picture questions about your essay.

Macro-Revision Questions:

QuestionWhat to Look For
Does the essay have a clear dominant impression?One word or phrase that captures the mood. Every detail should support it.
Is the organization logical?Spatial, chronological, or order of importance. Can the reader follow?
Does each paragraph have a topic sentence?A clear signpost for the reader.
Does each paragraph have unity?Every sentence supports the paragraph’s main idea.
Is there a clear opening that hooks the reader?Introduces person/place and dominant impression.
Is there a conclusion that reinforces without repeating?Leaves the reader with a final image or reflection.

5. Sensory Audit: Are You Showing Enough?

A strong descriptive essay engages multiple senses. Too many writers rely only on sight.

The Sensory Audit Checklist:

6. Verb Strength: Replace Weak Verbs

Weak verbs (to be, to have, to do, to make) tell rather than show. Strong verbs create action and imagery.

Weak Verb Revisions:

WeakStrong
The river was cold.The river numbed my ankles.
There was a table in the corner.A wooden table occupied the corner.
She had a sad expression.Her face crumpled, and her eyes glistened.
The wind was strong.The wind rattled the windows and tore leaves from the trees.

Revision Strategy for Verbs:

  1. Circle every form of “to be” (is, am, are, was, were, be, being, been)
  2. Circle “there is/are/was/were” constructions
  3. For each circled word, ask: Can I replace this with an action verb?
  4. Rewrite the sentence.

7. Sentence Variety: Avoid Monotony

Too many sentences of the same length and structure create a boring rhythm.

Common Problems:

ProblemExampleFix
All short sentencesThe kitchen was warm. The windows were fogged. My grandmother stood at the counter.Combine or vary length
All starting with subjectShe rolled dough. She hummed. She smiled.Vary opening (prepositional phrases, dependent clauses)
Too many “and” connectionsShe walked to the store and bought milk and then came home and made dinner.Break into shorter sentences or restructure

Sentence Variety Techniques:

TechniqueExample
Start with a prepositional phrase“At the back of the room, a single lamp glowed.”
Start with a dependent clause“When she smiled, the lines around her eyes deepened.”
Invert normal order“On the windowsill grew scallions in tin cans.”
Combine short related sentences“The windows were fogged, and steam rose from the stove.”

8. Final Proofreading Checklist

After macro- and micro-revision, proofread for errors.

Proofreading Checklist:

CategoryWhat to Check
SpellingRun spell-check, but also read carefully (spell-check misses homonyms: their/there/they’re)
PunctuationCommas, periods, quotation marks, apostrophes
CapitalizationFirst word of sentences, proper nouns
Subject-verb agreementSingular subjects with singular verbs
Pronoun agreementClear antecedents
Sentence fragmentsEvery sentence must have a subject and verb
Run-onsTwo independent clauses without proper punctuation

Proofreading Strategy: Read your essay aloud. Your ear will catch mistakes your eye skips. Also try reading it backwards (last sentence to first) to focus on individual sentences rather than flow.

Exercise 1: Macro-Revision Practice

Instructions: Read this draft paragraph from a student essay about a grandfather. Identify macro-revision issues. Then suggest a reorganization or focus change.

My grandfather was a quiet man. He never said much at family dinners. He had a workshop in the basement where he fixed old radios. The basement smelled of dust and solder. His hands were always stained with grease. I remember sitting on a stool watching him work. He would hum sometimes, very softly. He died two years ago, and I still miss him. The radios are still down there, unfinished.

Questions to answer:

  1. What is the dominant impression? Is it consistent?
  1. Does the paragraph have unity? (What different ideas are mixed together?)
  1. How would you reorganize this material into two or three focused paragraphs?
