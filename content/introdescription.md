## Key Concepts

### 1. What Is Description?

**Definition:**
Descriptive writing uses carefully chosen words to create a vivid mental picture for the reader. It transforms flat facts into a living, breathing experience.

**Key Principle:**
Description answers not just "What is there?" but "What is it *like* to be there?"

**Example from George Orwell:**
In "Shooting an Elephant," Orwell writes:

> "The elephant was standing eight yards from the road, his left side towards us. He took not the slightest notice of the crowd's arrival. He was tearing up bunches of grass, beating them against his knees to clean them, and stuffing them into his mouth."

Instead of saying "The elephant was eating," Orwell shows us specific actions: tearing, beating, stuffing. This is the difference between telling and showing.

---

### 2. The Purpose of Description

Writers use description for four main purposes:

| Purpose | What It Does |
|---------|--------------|
| To Create Atmosphere | Establishes mood (fear, joy, peace, tension) |
| To Reveal Character | Shows personality through appearance and action |
| To Make Ideas Tangible | Turns abstract concepts into something we can perceive |
| To Control Pacing | Slows time for important moments |

**Connection to Chinese Poetry:**
In Li Bai's "Quiet Night Thought":

> Before my bed, the moon is shining bright —
> I think it's frost upon the ground.

Li Bai doesn't say "I feel homesick." He shows us moonlight mistaken for frost—a concrete image that evokes longing.

---

### 3. Concrete vs. Abstract Language

**This is the most important concept of Week 1.**

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Abstract Language** | Words for ideas, qualities, or concepts that cannot be perceived by the five senses | Love, hate, beauty, justice, sadness, freedom, success |
| **Concrete Language** | Words for things that CAN be perceived by the five senses | Rain, coffee, silk, thunder, lemon, rough wood, warm skin |

**The Golden Rule:**
> **Abstract language tells. Concrete language shows.**

**Compare These Examples:**

| Abstract | Concrete |
|----------|----------|
| She was beautiful. | Sunlight caught the copper in her hair. When she smiled, the lines around her eyes deepened. |
| The food was delicious. | Steam rose from the dumplings. The first bite released hot broth, pork, and ginger across my tongue. |
| He felt nervous. | His palms left damp marks on the podium. He cleared his throat four times before speaking. |

Which column makes you feel something? Which makes you see something?

**The Key Question to Ask Yourself:**
Whenever you write an abstract word, ask: **"What does that LOOK like?"**

---

### 4. Sensory Details

Good description engages multiple senses, not just sight.

| Sense | Questions to Ask | Strong Verbs/Adjectives |
|-------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Sight** | What colors? Shapes? Movements? Light or shadow? | Gleam, shimmer, loom, fade, jagged, hazy |
| **Sound** | Loud or soft? Constant or sudden? Near or far? | Whisper, rumble, creak, murmur, crash, hum |
| **Smell** | Pleasant or unpleasant? Strong or faint? Reminds you of what? | Pungent, sweet, musty, fragrant, acrid, stale |
| **Touch** | Texture? Temperature? Weight? Moisture? | Rough, silky, burning, icy, feather-light, clammy |
| **Taste** | Sweet, sour, bitter, salty, umami? (Can be metaphorical) | Tart, bitter, metallic, honeyed, peppery |

**The Sensory Pyramid:**
- Base: Sight (easiest, most common)
- Middle: Sound, Touch (add dimension)
- Peak: Smell, Taste (most evocative, connected to memory)

**Example: Multiple Senses in E.B. White:**
From "Once More to the Lake":

> "the motors made a petulant, irritable sound in the hot mornings and they whined about one's ears like mosquitoes at night in the still evening"

- **Sound:** "petulant, irritable sound," "whined"
- **Touch:** "hot mornings"
- **Sight implied:** "still evening"
- **Simile:** "like mosquitoes" (evokes both sound and the feeling of being bitten)

**Example: Smell in Patrick Süskind:**
From *Perfume*:

> "The streets stank of manure, the courtyards of urine, the stairwells stank of moldering wood and rat droppings, the kitchens of spoiled cabbage and mutton fat."

The specificity ("moldering wood," "spoiled cabbage") makes the description visceral and unforgettable.

---

### 5. The Dominant Impression

**Definition:**
The **dominant impression** is the single mood, feeling, or atmosphere that a descriptive passage creates. Every detail should contribute to this unified effect.

**The Principle of Selection:**
> A writer does not include everything. A writer selects details that support the dominant impression and omits details that contradict it.

**Example: Decay in Faulkner:**
From "A Rose for Emily":

> "It was a big, squarish frame house that had once been white... lifting its stubborn and coquettish decay above the cotton wagons and the gasoline pumps—an eyesore among eyesores."

**Dominant impression:** Faded glory, stubborn decay
**Supporting details:** "had once been white," "encroached," "obliterated," "decay," "eyesore"

**Example: Energy in Dillard:**
From *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek*:

> "The fish were jumping as if possessed... in a perfect frenzy, as if the sea were a fiery furnace and they were trying to leap to safety."

**Dominant impression:** Frenzied energy, desperate vitality
**Supporting details:** "as if possessed," "churning," "gunshot," "frenzy," "fiery furnace"

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Description** | Writing that creates vivid mental images |
| **Concrete Language** | Words for things we can perceive through senses |
| **Abstract Language** | Words for ideas and feelings |
| **Sensory Detail** | Information appealing to sight, sound, smell, touch, or taste |
| **Dominant Impression** | The single mood created by selected details |
| **Show, Don't Tell** | The principle of using concrete details rather than abstract statements |

## Homework Reminder

**Task:** Write a one-paragraph description of a place from memory.

**Requirements:**
- Length: 8-12 sentences
- One clear dominant impression
- At least three different senses
- Concrete language only—no abstract statements about feelings
- Do NOT state the impression directly

**Options:**
- Your childhood home
- A grandparent's house
- A favorite market or street
- A temple or park
- Your current dorm room

**Structure Suggestion:**
- Sentence 1-2: Establish the place (where, when)
- Sentence 3-9: Concrete, sensory details (show the impression)
- Sentence 10-12: Closing that reinforces the impression
