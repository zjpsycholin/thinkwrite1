## Key Concepts

### 1. The Revision Mindset: Macro vs. Micro

**Definition:**
Revision means "seeing again." It is not simply fixing typos or correcting grammar. It is re-envisioning your essay to make it clearer, more vivid, and more coherent.

**Two Levels of Revision:**

| Level | Focus | Questions to Ask |
|-------|-------|------------------|
| **Macro-revision** | Big picture: structure, focus, dominant impression, paragraph order, evidence | Does the essay have a clear dominant impression? Is the organization logical? Does every paragraph serve a purpose? |
| **Micro-revision** | Sentence level: word choice, imagery, transitions, grammar, punctuation | Is every word precise? Are there clichés? Do sentences flow? |

**The Revision Process:**
Write Draft → Set Aside (few hours or a day) → Macro-revision → Micro-revision → Proofread → Final Draft


---

### 2. Strengthening Imagery: From Generic to Specific

Weak imagery relies on generic nouns, weak verbs, and clichés. Strong imagery uses precise, sensory language.

**The Specificity Ladder:**

| Generic | Better | Strongest (Specific + Sensory) |
|---------|--------|-------------------------------|
| A bird | A sparrow | A house sparrow with its chest puffed against the cold |
| She laughed | She giggled | She let out a laugh that crackled like dry leaves |
| The room was messy | The room was cluttered | Clothes carpeted the floor; empty cups towered on the desk |
| He walked | He shuffled | He shuffled, his slippers scuffing the linoleum |

**Example from Literature:**
From Tim O'Brien's *The Things They Carried*:

> "He carried a strobe light and a pair of marine binoculars. He carried a .45-caliber pistol that weighed 2.9 pounds fully loaded. He carried a compass in his rucksack and a wristwatch with a luminous dial."

O'Brien could have said "He carried military equipment." Instead, he gives specific weights, brands, and details. We see and feel each item.

**Revision Strategy for Weak Imagery:**
1. Circle every generic noun (bird, food, building, person, thing)
2. Ask: What specific kind? What does it look/sound/smell like?
3. Replace with a concrete, sensory phrase.

---

### 3. Eliminating Clichés and Abstractions

**Clichés** are phrases that have been used so often they no longer create a vivid image.

| Cliché | Why It Fails |
|--------|--------------|
| "busy as a bee" | Readers don't picture a bee; they just register "busy" |
| "cold as ice" | No fresh image |
| "heart of gold" | Abstract and vague |
| "time stood still" | Overused, loses impact |

**How to Revise Clichés:**
- Ask: What is a fresh way to say this?
- Get specific: Instead of "busy as a bee," show what the person actually does.
- Use original comparison: Instead of "cold as ice," describe the specific sensation of cold.

**Abstract vs. Concrete:**

| Abstract | Concrete Revision |
|----------|-------------------|
| He was nervous | His palms left damp marks on the podium. |
| The food was delicious | Steam rose from the dumplings. The first bite released hot broth, pork, and ginger across my tongue. |
| She felt lonely | She ate dinner alone at a table for two. The chair across from her stayed empty. |

---

### 4. Macro-Revision Checklist for Descriptive Essays

Before focusing on sentences, answer these big-picture questions about your essay.

**Macro-Revision Questions:**

| Question | What to Look For |
|----------|------------------|
| Does the essay have a clear **dominant impression**? | One word or phrase that captures the mood. Every detail should support it. |
| Is the **organization logical**? | Spatial, chronological, or order of importance. Can the reader follow? |
| Does each **paragraph have a topic sentence**? | A clear signpost for the reader. |
| Does each paragraph have **unity**? | Every sentence supports the paragraph's main idea. |
| Is there a clear **opening** that hooks the reader? | Introduces person/place and dominant impression. |
| Is there a **conclusion** that reinforces without repeating? | Leaves the reader with a final image or reflection. |

---

### 5. Sensory Audit: Are You Showing Enough?

A strong descriptive essay engages multiple senses. Too many writers rely only on sight.

**The Sensory Audit Checklist:**

| Sense | Questions | Check if used |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| Sight | What colors, shapes, movements, light? | ☐ |
| Sound | What can be heard? Loud or soft? Constant or sudden? | ☐ |
| Smell | What odors? Pleasant or unpleasant? Strong or faint? | ☐ |
| Touch | What textures, temperatures, weights? | ☐ |
| Taste | Any tastes (literal or metaphorical)? | ☐ |

---

### 6. Verb Strength: Replace Weak Verbs

Weak verbs (to be, to have, to do, to make) tell rather than show. Strong verbs create action and imagery.

**Weak Verb Revisions:**

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| The river was cold. | The river numbed my ankles. |
| There was a table in the corner. | A wooden table occupied the corner. |
| She had a sad expression. | Her face crumpled, and her eyes glistened. |
| The wind was strong. | The wind rattled the windows and tore leaves from the trees. |

**Revision Strategy for Verbs:**
1. Circle every form of "to be" (is, am, are, was, were, be, being, been)
2. Circle "there is/are/was/were" constructions
3. For each circled word, ask: Can I replace this with an action verb?
4. Rewrite the sentence.

---

### 7. Sentence Variety: Avoid Monotony

Too many sentences of the same length and structure create a boring rhythm.

**Common Problems:**

| Problem | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| All short sentences | The kitchen was warm. The windows were fogged. My grandmother stood at the counter. | Combine or vary length |
| All starting with subject | She rolled dough. She hummed. She smiled. | Vary opening (prepositional phrases, dependent clauses) |
| Too many "and" connections | She walked to the store and bought milk and then came home and made dinner. | Break into shorter sentences or restructure |

**Sentence Variety Techniques:**

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Start with a prepositional phrase | "At the back of the room, a single lamp glowed." |
| Start with a dependent clause | "When she smiled, the lines around her eyes deepened." |
| Invert normal order | "On the windowsill grew scallions in tin cans." |
| Combine short related sentences | "The windows were fogged, and steam rose from the stove." |

---

### 8. Final Proofreading Checklist

After macro- and micro-revision, proofread for errors.

**Proofreading Checklist:**

| Category | What to Check |
|----------|---------------|
| **Spelling** | Run spell-check, but also read carefully (spell-check misses homonyms: their/there/they're) |
| **Punctuation** | Commas, periods, quotation marks, apostrophes |
| **Capitalization** | First word of sentences, proper nouns |
| **Subject-verb agreement** | Singular subjects with singular verbs |
| **Pronoun agreement** | Clear antecedents |
| **Sentence fragments** | Every sentence must have a subject and verb |
| **Run-ons** | Two independent clauses without proper punctuation |

> **Proofreading Strategy:**
> Read your essay **aloud**. Your ear will catch mistakes your eye skips. Also try reading it **backwards** (last sentence to first) to focus on individual sentences rather than flow.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Macro-revision** | Big-picture changes to structure, focus, and organization |
| **Micro-revision** | Sentence-level changes to word choice, imagery, and grammar |
| **Specificity** | Using precise, concrete nouns instead of generic ones |
| **Cliché** | An overused phrase that no longer creates a vivid image |
| **Abstract language** | Words that name ideas or feelings without showing them |
| **Concrete language** | Words that appeal to the five senses |
| **Sensory audit** | Checking an essay for use of sight, sound, smell, touch, and taste |
| **Sentence variety** | Mixing sentence lengths and structures for better rhythm |
