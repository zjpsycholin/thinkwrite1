## Key Concepts

### 1. What Is "Show, Don't Tell"?

**Definition:**
"Show, don't tell" is the principle of presenting details, actions, and sensations that allow readers to draw their own conclusions—rather than stating those conclusions directly.



**The Difference:**

| Telling (Abstract) | Showing (Concrete) |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| He was angry. | His jaw tightened. His fists clenched at his sides. When he spoke, his voice came out in a low growl. |
| She felt nervous. | She twisted her hands in her lap. Her eyes darted toward the door every few seconds. She cleared her throat three times before speaking. |
| The room was cozy. | A fire crackled in the hearth. Soft blankets draped the worn armchair. Steam rose from a mug on the side table. |

**Why It Matters:**
- **Respects the reader:** Allows readers to participate in creating meaning
- **Creates engagement:** Readers experience the story rather than just receiving information
- **Builds credibility:** Concrete details feel more truthful and persuasive
- **Evokes emotion:** Readers feel what characters feel rather than being told about it

---

### 2. The Problem with Telling

**Common Telling Traps:**

| Trap | Example | Why It Fails |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Emotion labels** | "She was sad." | Tells us what to feel without making us feel it |
| **Judgment statements** | "He was a bad person." | Imposes author's opinion without evidence |
| **Summary statements** | "They had a wonderful time." | Skims over experience instead of immersing us |
| **Abstract praise** | "The view was beautiful." | Generic; doesn't create a specific image |

**Why Beginning Writers Tell?**    


**Example Transformation:**

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| He was exhausted after the long day. | He dropped his bag by the door and let himself fall onto the couch, not bothering to remove his shoes. His eyes stayed half-closed as the television murmured to itself. When his phone buzzed, he didn't reach for it. |

**Consider:** Which version makes you *feel* the exhaustion? Which version just reports it?

### 3. How to Show: Emotions Through Action

The most powerful way to show emotion is through **physical action and reaction**.

**Emotion-to-Action Chart:**

| Emotion | Physical Signs | Actions |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Anger** | Clenched jaw, red face, tense shoulders | Slams doors, speaks through teeth, throws objects |
| **Fear** | Wide eyes, trembling, pale skin | Backs away, freezes, voice shakes, looks for escape |
| **Joy** | Smiling eyes, relaxed posture, laughter | Jumps, hugs, dances, repeats happy news |
| **Sadness** | Drooping shoulders, tear-filled eyes, slow movements | Withdraws, stares at nothing, speaks little |
| **Nervousness** | Sweating, fidgeting, avoiding eye contact | Clears throat, checks phone repeatedly, bites nails |
| **Love** | Soft eyes, gentle touch, leaning toward person | Remembers small details, prepares thoughtful gifts |

Example from Literature: Anton Chekhov    
Chekhov, a master of showing, wrote:

> "The people at the supper table ate in silence. The food was tasteless. The wine seemed warm. They wanted to leave but didn't know how."

**What did Chekhov did and what did Chekhov not do?**  


**Example from Literature: Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants"**

Hemingway shows tension between a couple almost entirely through dialogue and action:

> "The woman brought two glasses of beer and two felt pads. She put the felt pads and the beer glasses on the table and looked at the man and the girl. The girl was looking off at the line of hills. They were white in the sun and the country was brown and dry."
>
> "They look like white elephants," she said.
>
> "I've never seen one," the man drank his beer.
>
> "No, you wouldn't have."
>
> "I might have," the man said. "Just because you say I wouldn't have doesn't prove anything."


**What does Hemingway show us?**

### 4. The Showing Mindset

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

Instead of asking "What is my character feeling?" ask:
- **What would someone DO if they felt this way?**
- **What would their body look like?**
- **What would they say, and how would they say it?**
- **What would they NOT do that they normally would?**

**The Subtraction Method:**
Ask yourself: If I removed all emotion words from this passage, would a reader still understand what my character feels?

{{< expand "Example" >}}
**Telling version:** "She was heartbroken when he left."

**Subtract emotion words:** Remove "heartbroken."

**Showing version:** "After he left, she sat on the edge of the bed, still holding his pillow. The door clicked shut. She didn't move for a long time. When she finally stood, she walked to the window, but he was already gone. She pressed her forehead to the cold glass and closed her eyes."
{{< /expand >}}

### 5. Showing Through Dialogue

Dialogue is one of the most powerful tools for showing. What characters say—and how they say it—reveals emotion, relationship, and conflict.

**Dialogue Techniques:**

| Technique | What It Shows | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **What they say** | Direct expression of thoughts | "I don't want to talk about it." |
| **What they don't say** | Avoidance, tension, secrets | Long pause. Subject change. |
| **How they speak** | Emotional state | Short sentences = anger. Trailing off = uncertainty. |
| **What they repeat** | Obsession, worry, importance | "Are you sure? Are you absolutely sure?" |
| **Interruptions** | Excitement, disrespect, urgency | "But I—" "No, listen to me first." |

{{< expand "Example: Dialogue Showing Tension" >}}
From Raymond Carver's "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love":

> "Do you love me?" I asked.
>
> "Yes," she said.
>
> "Do you really love me?"
>
> "I said yes."
>
> "But do you really love me?"
>
> She put down her glass and looked at me. "What's wrong with you?" she said. "I said yes. I said it twice."

Carver never says "He was insecure" or "She was frustrated." The dialogue shows us everything.
{{< /expand >}}

**Example: Dialogue Showing Character**

From Amy Tan's *The Joy Luck Club*:

> "Only ask you be your best, for your sake," my mother said in Chinese. "In America, you can be anything you want to be. You just have to work hard."

The dialogue reveals the mother's values, her immigrant hope, and her love—without ever saying "She loved her daughter."

### 6. Showing Through Body Language and Gesture

Physical details often reveal more than words.

**The Body Language Spectrum:**

| Emotion | Body Language |
|---------|---------------|
| **Confidence** | Upright posture, steady eye contact, open gestures |
| **Insecurity** | Hunched shoulders, avoiding eyes, touching face/neck |
| **Interest** | Leaning forward, mirrored gestures, dilated pupils |
| **Disinterest** | Leaning away, checking phone, glancing at door |
| **Deception** | Touching mouth, shifting weight, inconsistent gestures |

Example from Literature: Tim O'Brien
From *The Things They Carried*:

> "Lieutenant Jimmy Cross carried letters from a girl named Martha... In the late afternoon, after a day's march, he would dig his foxhole, wash his hands under a canteen, unwrap the letters, hold them with the tips of his fingers, and spend the last hour of light pretending."

The detail "hold them with the tips of his fingers" shows reverence, tenderness, longing—without a single abstract word.


**Example: Contrasting Body Language**

Consider these two descriptions:

> He sat across from her at the café.

Now with body language showing his feelings:

> He sat across from her at the café, leaning forward, elbows on the table. When she spoke, he nodded, his eyes never leaving her face. His coffee grew cold, untouched.

What do we understand about his feelings? (Interest, attraction, attention)

---

### 7. Showing Through Action and Reaction

Characters reveal themselves most clearly through what they **do**—especially in moments of stress or choice.

**The Action Spectrum:**

| Type of Action | What It Shows |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Habitual actions** | Personality, routine, comfort zones |
| **Choices under pressure** | True character, values, priorities |
| **Reactions to others** | Relationships, emotions, hidden feelings |
| **What they notice** | Obsessions, fears, desires |
| **What they ignore** | Priorities, biases, limitations |

Example from Literature: James Joyce"
From "Araby," Joyce shows a boy's crush through his actions:

> "Every morning I lay on the floor in the front parlor watching her door. The blind was pulled down to within an inch of the sash so that I could not be seen. When she came out on the doorstep my heart leaped. I ran to the hall, seized my books and followed her."

Joyce never says "He was infatuated." He shows us: watching her door, hiding, heart leaping, rushing to follow.


**Example: Showing Through Contrast**

What does this action reveal?

> The phone rang. He stared at it, watching the screen light up with her name. He didn't move. After the sixth ring, it stopped. He picked up the phone, looked at the missed call, and set it back down without calling back.

What do we understand? (Conflict, avoidance, possibly a broken relationship)

---

### 8. The "Camera Lens" Technique

Imagine your writing is a camera. To show instead of tell, you can:

**Zoom in:**
- From general to specific
- From room to face to trembling hand

**Follow action:**
- Capture movement in real time
- Show the sequence of small actions

**Capture sensory details:**
- What would the camera record?
- Sight, sound, movement—not thoughts or feelings

{{< expand "Example: Zoom In" >}}
**Telling:** She was nervous about the interview.

**Camera lens showing:**
> She smoothed her skirt for the fifth time. At the door, she paused, took a breath, and reached for the handle. Her palm was damp. She wiped it on her jacket before knocking.
{{< /expand >}}

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Show, Don't Tell** | The principle of presenting details that allow readers to draw conclusions rather than stating those conclusions directly |
| **Telling** | Stating emotions, judgments, or summaries directly |
| **Showing** | Revealing through action, dialogue, body language, and sensory details |
| **Emotion Label** | A word that names a feeling (happy, sad, angry) |
| **Body Language** | Physical gestures, posture, and movements that reveal emotion |
| **Camera Lens Technique** | Zooming in on specific, observable details as if recording with a camera |

---

## Common Telling Words to Avoid

When revising, watch for these words—they often signal telling:

| Emotion Words | Judgment Words | Summary Words |
|--------------|----------------|---------------|
| happy, sad, angry, nervous, excited, scared, lonely, embarrassed, guilty, proud | beautiful, ugly, wonderful, terrible, good, bad, nice, mean, kind | interesting, boring, fun, difficult, easy, strange, normal |

Whenever you use these, ask: **"What does this LOOK like?"**
