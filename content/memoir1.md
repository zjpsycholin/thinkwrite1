## 3. Interview Order vs Narrative Order

An interview often follows the order of conversation. The speaker answers questions, moves between topics, remembers new details, and reflects freely.

A narrative needs a different kind of order.

| Interview Order | Narrative Order |
|---|---|
| Follows questions and answers | Follows experience and meaning |
| Moves freely between topics | Builds clear progression |
| Includes many facts and memories | Selects useful materials |
| May repeat ideas | Avoids unnecessary repetition |
| Explains information | Recreates experience |
| Depends on the interviewer’s questions | Depends on the writer’s purpose |

---

## 4. Five Structure Options for This Project

You may choose a structure according to your selected materials.

| Structure | How It Works | Best For This Interview |
|---|---|---|
| **Chronological structure** | Events are told in time order | Childhood theatre → acting career → illness → recovery |
| **Turning-point structure** | The story builds toward one major change | First aneurysm, second aneurysm, father’s death |
| **Before-and-after structure** | The narrative contrasts past and present | Before and after illness / before and after grief |
| **Thematic structure** | Several moments are connected by one idea | Failure, fear, fame, self-care, authenticity |
| **Frame structure** | Present interview reflection frames past memory | Begins with interview voice, moves into a past scene, returns to reflection |

---

## 5. Understanding Each Structure

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin: 1em 0;">

### 1. Chronological Structure

This structure follows time order.

```text
childhood passion → early acting → sudden fame → illness → recovery → reflection
````

This works if your narrative direction is **a journey of growth**.

Possible focus:

> How Clarke’s early love of performance led her into a career that later tested her body, identity, and sense of self.

</div>

---

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin: 1em 0;">

### 2. Turning-Point Structure

This structure builds toward one major crisis or realization.

```text
background → pressure increases → turning point → aftermath → reflection
```

This works if your narrative direction is **a struggle and response**.

Possible turning points:

* the first aneurysm at the gym;
* the second aneurysm and surgery;
* the death of her father;
* realizing that recovery was not just physical.

</div>

---

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin: 1em 0;">

### 3. Before-and-After Structure

This structure creates contrast.

```text
Before: ambition, work, public success
After: illness, grief, fear, new understanding
```

This works if your narrative direction is **personal transformation**.

Possible focus:

> Before the illness, success meant working harder. After the illness, recovery required learning how to care for herself.

</div>

---

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin: 1em 0;">

### 4. Thematic Structure

This structure connects several moments through one repeated idea.

```text
failure in maths → failure in recovery → failure in fame → new understanding of failure
```

This works well because the interview itself is organized around “failures.”

Possible focus:

> The story is not about failure as defeat, but failure as a path toward self-knowledge.

</div>

---

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin: 1em 0;">

### 5. Frame Structure

This structure begins in the present, moves into the past, and returns to present reflection.

```text
present interview moment → past memory → present meaning
```

This works well if you want to use the interview situation itself as part of the narrative.

Possible opening:

> When the interviewer asked about recovery, Clarke did not describe survival as simple victory. She called recovery a failure.

</div>

## 2. Point of View in Interview-Based Memoir Writing

Because this project is based on someone else’s interview, point of view is important.

| Point of View               | Strength                                | Risk                                                        |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **First person: I**         | More personal and memoir-like           | May sound as if you are inventing Clarke’s private thoughts |
| **Third person: she**       | More objective and safer for nonfiction | May feel less intimate                                      |
| **Reflective third person** | Allows interpretation and reflection    | Needs careful control of tone                               |

---

### First Person

First person can sound intimate.

Example:

> I kept telling myself I had to get back to work.

But first person is risky because you are not Emilia Clarke. If you use “I,” your writing may sound as if you are speaking directly for her.

Use first person only if:

* you clearly base it on the transcript;
* you do not invent unsupported private thoughts;
* you understand that this is a reconstructed memoir voice.

---

### Third Person

Third person is safer for interview-based nonfiction.

Example:

> Clarke kept trying to return to work, even while her body was asking for rest.

Third person allows you to stay close to the interview while still creating a narrative.

---

### Reflective Third Person

Reflective third person combines narration and interpretation.

Example:

> Years later, Clarke would describe recovery not as triumph, but as a failure to give herself kindness.

This is useful when the interview already includes reflection on past events.

## 4. Scene, Summary, and Reflection

A memoir-style nonfiction narrative usually needs three elements.

```text
scene + summary + reflection
```

| Element        | Function                                    | Example from This Interview                                    |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scene**      | Slows time down and shows a specific moment | the gym, the hospital, the flight home, Comic-Con              |
| **Summary**    | Moves time forward and gives background     | acting career, recovery period, public fame                    |
| **Reflection** | Explains meaning                            | recovery as failure, fame as pressure, grief as transformation |

---

## Example

### Summary

> Clarke had already experienced sudden fame when her health crisis began.

### Scene

> In the gym, she stopped during a plank. The pain felt like pressure snapping across her brain.

### Reflection

> The crisis did not simply interrupt her career; it changed the way she understood strength, work, and survival.

## Drafting a Narrative Opening

<div style="border-left: 5px solid #e67e22; padding: 1em; background-color: #fff7ef; margin: 1em 0;">

**Mode:** Individual writing
**Goal:** Draft a possible opening paragraph for your nonfiction narrative.

</div>

---

## Purpose of the Opening

A good opening should do at least one of the following:

* introduce the central person;
* create a vivid image;
* suggest conflict or tension;
* introduce the narrative direction;
* raise a question;
* begin with a scene;
* begin with reflection;
* connect the present interview to a past experience.

---

## Opening Options Based on the Transcript

| Opening Type                | How It Works                             | Possible Transcript Material            |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Scene opening**           | Begins with a specific moment            | gym, hospital, airplane, Comic-Con      |
| **Reflective opening**      | Begins with meaning                      | recovery, failure, fame, grief          |
| **Contrast opening**        | Shows public success vs private struggle | Game of Thrones fame vs brain injury    |
| **Interview-frame opening** | Begins with the interview situation      | “When asked about recovery…”            |
| **Image opening**           | Begins with a repeated image             | butterfly, stage, hospital room, camera |

---

## Writing Task

Write one opening paragraph for your narrative.

Your paragraph should include:

* a clear point of view;
* one specific image, action, or reflection;
* a hint of narrative direction;
* a connection to the interview material;
* a suggestion of conflict, tension, or change.

<div style="background-color: #fff8e6; border-left: 5px solid #f39c12; padding: 1em; margin: 1em 0;">

There is no fixed word limit.
The paragraph should be developed enough to show your plan.

</div>
