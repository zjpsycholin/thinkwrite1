Memoir Writing Project · Stage 2

Learning Narrative Skills through Draft Sharing

Project Stage: From one-experience draft to two-experience memoir draft
Class Time: 2 × 45 minutes
Group Mode: Groups of three
In-Class Focus: Sharing, discussion, teacher input, guided revision
After-Class Product: Add a second experience to the memoir draft

🎯 Stage 2 Goals

By the end of Stage 2, you should be able to:

  1. Share and discuss your Stage 1 one-experience draft.
  2. Identify strengths and weaknesses in narrative writing.
  3. Understand memoir writing as a form of narrative writing.
  4. Improve your use of scene, summary, character, tension, and reflection.
  5. Give useful comments on your own and your classmates’ drafts.
  6. Add a second experience to your memoir with stronger narrative control.

Part I · Reading Stage 1 Drafts as Narrative Writing

1. Group Sharing: Read One Experience Aloud

Each student reads the Stage 1 one-experience draft aloud.

While listening to your classmates, do not only focus on grammar. Listen for narrative writing skills.

While ListeningQuestions to Consider
ExperienceWhat experience is being narrated?
SceneIs there a specific moment, or is it only summary?
CharacterHow is Clarke shown through action, choice, or reaction?
TensionWhat pressure, conflict, fear, shame, or uncertainty appears?
ReflectionWhat meaning does the writer give to the experience?
Interview useDoes the writing stay close to the interview?

2. Peer Response: First Round

After each reading, group members should give useful comments.

Use the following sentence patterns:

The clearest experience in your draft is ...

The strongest narrative detail is ...

I can see tension when ...

One place that still feels too general is ...

One sentence that could be developed into a scene is ...

The reflection could become stronger if ...

Peer response rule

Do not only say “good” or “interesting.” Point to a specific sentence, detail, or moment in your classmate’s draft.

Part II · Memoir Is Not a Summary of Life

A memoir is based on real experience, but it is not a complete biography.

A memoir does not try to tell everything.

A memoir selects important experiences and gives them shape.

Memoir vs Biography vs Interview Summary

Type of WritingMain PurposeOrganizationWriter’s Job
BiographyTell the story of a person’s lifeOften chronologicalCover important life events
Interview summaryReport what the speaker saidFollows interview contentPresent information clearly
Memoir-style narrativeExplore the meaning of selected experiencesShaped by theme and reflectionSelect, arrange, and interpret experience

Key idea

A memoir is not “everything that happened.” A memoir is “what this experience means.”

Example: Summary vs Memoir-Style Narrative

Interview-summary style

Clarke talks about her brain aneurysm, her recovery, her father’s death, and her difficulty with fame.

This is clear, but it is not yet narrative writing.

Memoir-style narrative

In the gym, Clarke was still close to the beginning of her public life when pain suddenly cut through her body. The moment did not only threaten her health. It also forced her to understand how fragile success could be.

This version:

  • begins with a specific moment;
  • creates tension;
  • connects public success with private fear;
  • suggests meaning.

Part III · The Basic Unit of Memoir: Experience + Meaning

A useful memoir passage often has two parts:

experience + meaning

The experience answers:

What happened?

The meaning answers:

Why does it matter?

Experience without Meaning

Clarke had a brain aneurysm after filming the first season of Game of Thrones.

This gives information, but it does not yet create memoir meaning.

Meaning without Experience

This shows that life is unpredictable and people should be strong.

This gives a general idea, but it is not grounded in a concrete moment.

Experience + Meaning

After filming the first season of Game of Thrones, Clarke was doing a plank in the gym when a sudden pain struck her. The crisis matters not only because it threatened her life, but because it placed public success beside private vulnerability.

Writing principle

A memoir passage becomes stronger when a concrete experience leads to a specific meaning.

Part IV · Key Narrative Skills for Adding a Second Experience

Main purpose: You will learn how to add a second experience that connects meaningfully with the first experience.

Skill 1 · Scene and Summary

A memoir needs both scene and summary.

SceneSummary
Slows time downMoves time forward
Shows one specific momentCovers background or longer periods
Uses action, detail, speech, thought, settingExplains information efficiently
Helps readers experience the momentHelps readers understand context

When Should You Use a Scene?

Use a scene when the moment is:

  • emotionally important;
  • full of conflict or pressure;
  • a turning point;
  • vivid and concrete;
  • useful for showing character;
  • central to your theme.

When Should You Use Summary?

Use summary when you need to:

  • explain background;
  • move across time quickly;
  • connect two experiences;
  • avoid unnecessary detail;
  • give context before or after a scene.

Skill 2 · Character Through Action

In narrative writing, character should not be explained only through adjectives.

Avoid writing only:

Clarke was brave.
Clarke was afraid.
Clarke was hardworking.
Clarke was sad.

Instead, show character through:

  • what she does;
  • what she says;
  • what she notices;
  • how she reacts;
  • what she tries to hide;
  • what she repeats;
  • what she chooses.

From Label to Action

General LabelNarrative Action
Clarke was afraid.She kept repeating lines, trying to prove to herself that her mind was still working.
Clarke was ashamed.She wanted to go back to work before anyone could decide she was broken.
Clarke was grieving.On the flight home, she imagined herself as a butterfly moving toward the hospital room.
Clarke was exhausted by fame.She smiled for cameras while her body was still fighting fatigue.
Clarke was learning self-kindness.Years later, she could name recovery as the failure of not giving herself grace.

Practice: Improve the Sentence

Revise the following sentences.

Try to show character through action, image, or reaction.

1. Clarke was afraid.

2. Clarke loved her father.

3. Clarke felt ashamed after the illness.

4. Clarke had difficulty with fame.

5. Clarke learned that recovery was difficult.

Skill 3 · Tension: What Makes Readers Care?

Tension means that something matters and the outcome is uncertain.

A narrative becomes stronger when readers feel pressure.

Tension Formula

Character wants something + obstacle + risk = tension

Examples from Clarke’s Experiences

ExperienceWantObstacleRisk
Brain aneurysmShe wants to survive and keep her mindHer brain injuryDeath, damage, loss of identity
Hospital after injuryShe wants to return to workMedical crisis and shameBeing seen as weak or broken
Flight homeShe wants to reach her fatherDistance and no communicationMissing goodbye
Comic-ConShe wants to perform publiclyHeadache and fearDying in public / losing control
FameShe wants to remain herselfCelebrity expectationsLosing authenticity

Skill 4 · Reflection: Turning Experience into Meaning

Reflection explains why the experience matters.

Without reflection, the writing may feel like a report.

With reflection, the writing becomes memoir.

Weak Reflection

This was an important experience.

This is too general.

Stronger Reflection

This moment matters because it shows that success did not protect Clarke from fear, shame, or physical vulnerability.

Reflection Patterns

You may use these sentence patterns:

This experience matters because ...

The moment shows not only ..., but also ...

What changed was not only ..., but ...

For Clarke, this experience became a way to understand ...

The experience connects to the larger theme of ...

Practice: Add Reflection

Choose one sentence from your Stage 1 draft and add a reflection sentence after it.

Example:

Scene sentence:
At Comic-Con, Clarke felt the headache begin while cameras and fans surrounded her.

Reflection:
The moment shows the painful contrast between public success and private fear.

Part V · Connecting Two Experiences

The Stage 2 assignment asks you to add one more experience.

The second experience should not feel separate.

It should connect to the first experience.

Ways to Connect Two Experiences

Connection TypeHow It WorksExample
Same themeBoth experiences show the same ideabrain injury + fame = public success/private vulnerability
ContrastTwo experiences show differencechildhood stage comfort vs adult public pressure
DevelopmentSecond experience shows changeshame after illness → later self-kindness
RepetitionSimilar feeling appears againfear in hospital → fear at Comic-Con
Cause and effectOne experience leads to anotherillness → charity work
SymbolSame image connects experiencesbutterfly → grief and memory

Choosing Your Second Experience

Ask yourself:

Does this second experience connect to my first one?

Does it deepen the same theme?

Does it show change, contrast, or repetition?

Does it help readers understand Clarke more clearly?

Can I write it with scene, tension, and reflection?

After-Class Assignment · Stage 2

Add One More Experience to Your Memoir

After class, revise your Stage 1 draft and add one more experience from Emilia Clarke’s interview.

Your memoir draft should now include two experiences.

Assignment Instructions

You should submit:

  1. your revised first experience;
  2. your newly added second experience;
  3. a short reflection on how the two experiences connect.

Requirements

Your two-experience memoir draft should:

  • include two selected experiences from the interview;
  • use scene and summary appropriately;
  • show Clarke through action, choice, or reaction;
  • include tension, conflict, pressure, or change;
  • include reflection;
  • connect the two experiences clearly;
  • use interview material responsibly;
  • avoid inventing major facts or unsupported private thoughts.
