📖 Four-Week Narrative Writing Project

From Interview to Memoir: Writing a Nonfiction Narrative

Project focus: Interview-based nonfiction narrative
Writing mode: Group discussion + individual writing
Genre: Memoir-style nonfiction narrative
Final output: Narrative essay + writer’s reflection

🌱 Project Overview

This project is based on an assigned interview. You will use the interview as source material and develop it into a nonfiction narrative, similar to a memoir.

You will work in a group of three for discussion, planning, and feedback. However, your final writing will be completed individually.

🎬 Source Interview

This project is based on the following interview:

You will write an individual nonfiction narrative based on the assigned interview.

⭐ Core Principle

You should not simply summarize the interview.

Instead, you should select and organize important experiences, memories, conflicts, turning points, relationships, or reflections from the interview and shape them into a coherent nonfiction narrative.

A summary reports what the interview says.
A nonfiction narrative recreates experience, develops character, and shows meaning.

Your narrative may focus on:

  • several connected moments;
  • a life stage;
  • a turning point and its consequences;
  • a repeated struggle;
  • a relationship;
  • a journey of change;
  • the development of an idea, dream, or identity.

🔵 Week 2 Subtask

Organizing Your Narrative Structure

🎯 Main Goal

This week, you will organize the materials you selected from the interview into a clear nonfiction narrative.

If you use several moments, they should not feel like a loose summary. They need to be connected by a clear structure, point of view, and theme.

By the end of this week, you should know:

  • how your narrative begins;
  • how your selected materials are connected;
  • where the conflict, tension, or change develops;
  • which parts should become scenes;
  • which parts should be summarized;
  • how the narrative reaches reflection or meaning.

Step 1: Choose a Narrative Structure

Not all nonfiction narratives use the same structure. Choose the structure that best fits your materials.

StructureHow It WorksBest For
Chronological structureEvents are told in time orderGrowth, life stages, change over time
Turning-point structureThe narrative builds toward one major changeDecisions, crises, realizations
Before-and-after structureThe narrative contrasts past and presentPersonal transformation
Thematic structureSeveral moments are connected by one ideaRepeated struggle, identity, family, dreams
Frame structureThe narrative begins in the present, returns to the past, and ends in the presentMemory, reflection, interview-based writing

Step 3: Choose Point of View and Voice

Because this project is based on an interview, you need to choose point of view carefully.

Point of ViewAdvantageRisk
First personMore intimate and memoir-likeMay sound like you are inventing the speaker’s private thoughts
Third personMore objective and safer for nonfictionMay feel less personal
Reflective third personGood for interview-based narrativeRequires careful control of tone

You may use first person or third person, but you must remain faithful to the interview.

Avoid inventing major facts, emotions, private thoughts, or dialogue that are not supported by the interview.

Step 4: Plan Scene and Summary

A nonfiction narrative usually needs both scene and summary.

SceneSummary
Slows time downSpeeds time up
Shows a specific momentGives background information
Uses action, detail, and sometimes dialogueExplains context or connects events
Helps readers experience the storyHelps readers understand the story

Use scenes for important moments.
Use summary for background or transitions.

🟣 Week 3 Subtask

Developing Scenes, Character, Tension, and Reflection

🎯 Main Skill Focus

This week, you will practice:

  • scene development;
  • character through action;
  • dialogue or indirect speech;
  • tension and emotional impact;
  • reflection within narrative.

1. Scene Options

You do not need to write only one key scene. However, you should avoid turning every part of the interview into a full scene.

A strong nonfiction narrative usually balances three elements:

scene + summary + reflection
ElementFunctionQuestion
SceneSlows time down and shows a specific momentWhat happened in this moment?
SummaryMoves time forward and gives backgroundWhat does the reader need to know?
ReflectionExplains meaningWhy does this experience matter?

What Is a Scene?

A scene lets readers experience a moment directly.

A scene usually includes:

  • a specific time and place;
  • character action;
  • concrete detail;
  • tension or emotion;
  • dialogue or thought, if appropriate.

Use a scene when the moment:

  • shows conflict;
  • reveals character;
  • marks a change;
  • contains strong emotion;
  • supports the theme.

What Is Summary?

A summary explains information quickly.

Use summary when you need to cover:

  • background information;
  • a long period of time;
  • repeated experiences;
  • context from the interview;
  • transitions between scenes.

What Is Reflection?

Reflection explains the meaning of the experience.

Use reflection to show:

  • what changed;
  • what the speaker learned;
  • why the experience matters;
  • what larger theme the story suggests.

Example: Scene + Summary + Reflection

Summary only:

The speaker faced many difficulties before becoming more confident.

Scene:

She stood outside the room, holding the paper in both hands. The voices inside grew quiet. When the door opened, she looked down first, then slowly stepped in.

Reflection:

That moment mattered not because she was fully prepared, but because she entered before she felt ready.

Combined version:

For years, she had avoided speaking in front of others. But that afternoon, she stood outside the room, holding the paper in both hands. The voices inside grew quiet. When the door opened, she looked down first, then slowly stepped in. That moment mattered not because she was fully prepared, but because she entered before she felt ready.

Possible Scene Patterns

PatternDescriptionBest For
One central sceneOne important moment receives detailed treatmentA major decision, conflict, or turning point
Two contrast scenesAn earlier scene and a later scene show changeGrowth, transformation, before/after contrast
Opening scene + reflectionA vivid opening leads into explanationStarting with a strong image or action
Scene-summary-sceneA scene begins the story; summary connects time; another scene shows changeA longer process or journey
Interview frame + past sceneThe interview voice frames a past experienceConnecting present reflection with past memory

How to Choose

If your material mainly shows…Choose…
One powerful eventOne central scene
Change over timeTwo contrast scenes
A vivid opening imageOpening scene + reflection
A longer journeyScene-summary-scene
Strong interview reflectionInterview frame + past scene

2. Character Through Action

In nonfiction narrative, character should not be described only through abstract labels.

Instead of saying:

The speaker was determined.

You can show determination through action:

He returned to the desk after midnight and opened the notebook again.

Use action, gesture, silence, choice, and repeated behavior to reveal character.

Abstract StatementAction-Based Revision
He was determined.He returned to the desk after midnight and opened the notebook again.
She was afraid.She kept her hand on the door but did not push it open.
He felt disappointed.He folded the letter slowly and placed it back into the envelope.
She became confident.This time, she looked up before answering.

3. Dialogue and Interview-Based Writing

Because this project is based on an interview, you need to use dialogue carefully.

You may use three types of speech representation:

TypeUseCaution
Direct quotationExact words from the interviewUse only when wording is accurate
Indirect speechMeaning is known but exact wording is notSafest for nonfiction
Reconstructed dialoguePlausible but not exact speechUse minimally and responsibly

Recommended rule:

If you do not know the exact words, use indirect speech.

🟠 Week 4 Subtask

Completing, Revising, and Reflecting

🎯 Main Skill Focus

This week, you will practice:

  • full narrative development;
  • coherence across selected materials;
  • reflection and meaning-making;
  • peer review;
  • revision.

1. Coherence Check

If you use several moments, you need clear connections.

Possible connecting methods include:

  • repeated image;
  • repeated question;
  • chronological development;
  • contrast between past and present;
  • recurring conflict;
  • reflective narrator;
  • theme-based transitions.

Coherence Check

  1. What holds my narrative together?
  1. Are the selected moments connected by time, theme, conflict, or change?
  1. Does each section serve the main narrative direction?
  1. Is there any part that only summarizes the interview but does not support the narrative?
  1. Does the ending connect back to the beginning or main theme?

2. The “So What?” Test

Complete the following questions:

  1. What experiences does the narrative present?
  1. Why do these experiences matter?
  1. What changes across the narrative?
  1. What does the main character understand, lose, gain, or become?
  1. What might readers learn from this narrative?
  1. Which sentence best expresses the meaning of the narrative?

✅ Final Reminder

A strong memoir-style nonfiction narrative should not simply repeat interview information.

It should:

  • select meaningful material;
  • organize it clearly;
  • develop scenes;
  • reveal character;
  • show conflict, change, or tension;
  • include reflection;
  • help readers understand why the experience matters.
